About the Author

My name is Raj Choudhary, and I am a thalassemia warrior, author, and someone who has learned to find strength in life's challenges.

Living with thalassemia since childhood has been a journey filled with hospital visits, blood transfusions, uncertainties, and countless obstacles. Yet, these experiences have also taught me resilience, patience, gratitude, and the importance of never giving up.

Writing was never just about telling a story for me—it became a way to express emotions, share experiences, and give a voice to struggles that often remain unseen.

Why I Wrote This Book

There was a fear that stayed with me for years.

I had seen many people in the thalassemia ward who were receiving blood transfusions alongside me. We would sit together, talk, laugh, and share our struggles. But sometimes, when the next transfusion date arrived, they were no longer there. Some of them never came back.

Those moments left a deep impact on me. They made me realize how fragile life can be. For a few days, people remember us, but eventually life moves on and everything returns to its normal pace.

"Will my journey end this quietly?"

While lying in a hospital bed, I made a decision. If I ever had to say goodbye to this world, I would not do so silently. I wanted my story, my experiences, and my voice to remain behind.

That was the moment I started writing.

This book is written for every thalassemia warrior whose story deserves to be heard. It is written for the countless faces I have met in hospital wards, for the struggles I have witnessed, and for the lives that have touched mine over the years.

If this book can become a voice for even a few of them, then it has fulfilled its purpose.

— Raj Choudhary

Why Blood Donors Are Important for Thalassemia Patients

For most people, blood donation is a simple act of kindness that takes only a few minutes. For a person living with thalassemia, however, that donation can mean another chance to live, dream, and hope.

Thalassemia is a lifelong blood disorder that prevents the body from producing enough healthy hemoglobin. Because of this, many patients depend on regular blood transfusions every few weeks to survive.

Behind every successful transfusion is an unknown hero — a blood donor.

Most blood donors may never meet the person whose life they help save. They may never know the relief a family feels when compatible blood is available on time.

For thalassemia patients, blood donors are much more than volunteers. They are a lifeline. Every unit of donated blood carries hope, strength, and the possibility of another tomorrow.

Blood cannot be manufactured in a laboratory. It cannot be bought when there is a shortage. It can only come from generous people willing to help others.

To every blood donor, thank you. Your donation is more than blood—it is hope, courage, and life itself.

Vision to Eradicate Thalassemia

Today, India is often referred to as the thalassemia capital of the world, with more than 100,000 registered patients dependent on regular blood transfusions for survival. Yet, despite these numbers, many people have never even heard the word thalassemia.

Lack of awareness remains one of the biggest challenges. Even when families learn about the condition, not everyone responds in the right way. Some seek proper medical guidance, while others choose to hide the condition because of social stigma and fear.

This mindset must change.

The first step toward eradicating thalassemia is not medical—it is social.

We must create an environment where people feel comfortable discussing thalassemia openly instead of hiding it. Awareness is the foundation of prevention.

However, awareness alone is not enough.

If we truly want to see a future where thalassemia is significantly reduced—or even eradicated—in the next 10 to 20 years, action must begin today.

One of the most effective preventive measures is premarital screening. By identifying carriers before marriage and providing proper genetic counseling, countless cases of thalassemia major can be prevented.

For this vision to become reality, governments, healthcare institutions, and medical professionals must work together. With strong awareness programs, accessible screening facilities, and supportive public policies, we can empower people to make informed decisions.

The goal is not merely to treat thalassemia—it is to prevent future generations from having to endure the same lifelong challenges.

A thalassemia-free future may seem distant today, but every meaningful change begins with awareness, acceptance, and action.

— Raj Choudhary
